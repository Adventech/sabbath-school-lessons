**Read This Week’s Passage:** Luke 23:1–31

### A Compromised Conscience

The story of Herod Antipas is a sad one. If you’ve ever met someone with a compromised conscience, you can understand why. You’ve probably seen it before—they have some sense of what’s right, yet lack the courage to do it when it matters most. They’re caught between a sense of morality and a desire to live their own way. Herod’s case presents a dramatic version of this very struggle. He crossed paths with two of the most central and convicting figures in the Gospels: John the Baptist and Jesus of Nazareth. His first encounter was with John the Baptist, and it wasn’t exactly a pleasant one. Let’s examine the backstory.

John had the privilege of preparing the way for Jesus’ ministry. While his work was entirely Christ-centered, he wasn’t afraid to speak hard truths—like when he rebuked Herod for marrying his own brother’s wife. Herodias, now Herod’s wife, was enraged at John and waited for the right moment to strike. John had already been arrested, but Herod had taken no further action. He hoped John’s arrest would appease his wife while avoiding the political fallout of killing a prophet. His compromised conscience paralyzed him.

Sometime later, during a party, Herodias’ daughter danced for Herod. In a rash moment of indulgence, he vowed to give her anything she asked for. Prompted by her mother, she requested John’s head on a platter. Herod here faced a true dilemma. Though he feared John, he feared the public embarrassment of breaking his vow even more, so he gave in, executing the prophet that very day.

Soon after John’s death, Jesus’ ministry began to gain more notoriety. When Herod heard of this miracle-working Rabbi, he began to wonder if it could be John, risen from the dead. Clearly, his conscience was troubled. A few years later, Herod finally had the chance to meet Jesus face to face. Jesus had been brought before Pilate, and as soon as Pilate learned Jesus was under Herod’s jurisdiction, he seized the opportunity to pass the problem on to someone else.

When Jesus stood before him, Herod tried to engage Him in conversation. However, Jesus didn’t say a word. Herod hoped to see a miracle like the ones he had heard about, but Jesus didn’t respond to that either. Herod was offended by Jesus’ silence, yet he sensed there was something different about this Man. He wasn’t like the criminals Herod had dealt with before. Still haunted by John’s death, Herod wasn’t in any frame of mind to heed the Jewish leaders’ prompting and sentence Jesus to die. Ironically, his father—Herod the Great—had been the first to try to kill Jesus as a baby. But Herod Antipas wanted nothing to do with such a scene. His guilty soul couldn’t bear another execution, so he sent Jesus back to Pilate, stating that he found nothing wrong with Him.

One question lingers in my mind after reading this interaction: why didn’t Jesus say anything? He at least spoke to Pilate—so why total silence here?

Jesus was silent before Herod because He had already said everything He needed to say through His prophet, John. This teaches us a powerful lesson. When Jesus speaks through His prophets and His Word, the message must be heeded. Otherwise, when we meet Him face to face, He may have nothing more to say to us. He speaks out of love and invites us to respond—but the choice is ours.

This week’s lesson covers Jesus being brought before Pilate, Herod, and the crowd. We will also witness Him receiving His cross. Things were beginning to intensify, and Satan was pressing Jesus to stumble in any way he could.
