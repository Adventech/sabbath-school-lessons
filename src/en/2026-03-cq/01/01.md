**Read This Week’s Passage:** John 11:43—12:33

### Pulling the Fire Alarm

One day when I was in preschool, my dad came to pick me up and went to talk with my teacher. While they were talking, I slowly began to walk down the stairs toward the entrance. On my way down, I saw the fire alarm. I stopped and just stared. I thought to myself, _I wonder what would happen if I pulled that thing._ At that moment, I gave no real consideration to the consequences of the decision I was weighing. The battle of curiosity went on for a few moments. Then, I made the choice: _I’m going to find out what this thing will do._

As you probably guessed, a super loud alarm went off. Lights started flashing all over the building, everyone began running past me to go outside, and eventually, a big red fire truck showed up to greet me in the parking lot. I couldn’t believe it. I didn’t expect it to turn out that way. If I had known all that was going to happen, I never would have done it.

Then came the next wave of thoughts: impending doom. _This is going to get me in big trouble._ And it did! The car ride home only amplified the sense of dread, culminating in serious consequences at home.

Lazarus’ resurrection was the last major miracle Jesus performed during His public ministry. This miracle was the final provocation for the religious leaders, setting in motion a series of events that marked the closing scenes of Jesus’ life and eventually led to His death.

The difference between His story and mine is that He knew exactly what would happen after He resurrected Lazarus. He did it despite having that foreknowledge. Feelings of impending doom undoubtedly accompanied that awareness, and I believe this is one reason for His heightened emotions throughout the story. After Lazarus’ resurrection, Jesus began to use language that reflected His awareness that His days on earth were coming to an end.

The passage from John 11:43—12:33 presents an interesting contrast: increasing excitement among the people over what Jesus did and growing anxiety among the religious leaders over what they might lose if His popularity continued. This tension climaxed when Caiaphas spoke with prophetic authority, declaring that Jesus had to die. He asserted it would be good for the nation if Jesus died and that His death would bring unity and solidarity to God’s people. Caiaphas played a pivotal role in the death of the Messiah—ironically, the very One he and his nation had been waiting for.

Jesus felt this tension as well. He knew His time was running out. During this period, He repeatedly tried to break the news to His disciples:

- “She has kept this for the day of My burial. For the poor you have with you always, but Me you do not have always” (John 12:7, 8).
- “The hour has come that the Son of Man should be glorified. Most assuredly, I say to you, unless a grain of wheat falls into the ground and dies, it remains alone; but if it dies, it produces much grain.… Now My soul is troubled, and what shall I say? ‘Father, save Me from this hour’? But for this purpose I came to this hour” (John 12:23, 24, 27).
- “Now is the judgment of this world; now the ruler of this world will be cast out. And I, if I am lifted up from the earth, will draw all peoples to Myself” (John 12:31, 32).

Jesus pressed onward to His death, moved by a love stronger than the suffering ahead of Him. As He weighed His options, “Jesus did not consider heaven a place to be desired while we were lost.”<sup>1</sup> To abandon His children would have brought Him greater agony than even the cross.
