### inTerpret

Throughout history, human beings have sought answers to life’s greatest philosophical challenges: Why are we here? How can we be happy? What is the purpose of suffering? While preaching in Athens, the apostle Paul encountered two prominent Greek philosophical schools of thought: Epicureanism and Stoicism. Both groups had distinct beliefs about the world and human happiness. Epicureans believed the gods were distant and uninvolved in human affairs. They thought pleasure to be the highest good, and their goal was to minimize pain and fear—especially the fear of death. They dismissed the idea of an afterlife or divine involvement. On the other hand, Stoics held a pantheistic view of the gods, seeing the divine as a rational force that permeated all of nature. They emphasized self-control, reason, and virtue as the keys to happiness and believed that aligning with nature’s reason was the way to achieve peace of mind.

While these philosophies tried to offer answers, they were incomplete. Jesus is the ultimate answer to the dilemmas  these schools of thought attempted to resolve. In Colossians 2:8–10, Paul addressed the limitations of human philosophy and emphasized that true wisdom is found in Christ: “Beware lest anyone cheat you through philosophy and empty deceit, according to the tradition of men, according to the basic principles of the world, and not according to Christ. For in Him dwells all the fullness of the Godhead bodily; and you are complete in Him, who is the head of all principality and power.” Philosophy without Christ cannot give eternal life or deep satisfaction and peace. Only Christ can give the fullest meaning and purpose to your life.

For the Epicureans, who believed the gods were distant and impersonal, Paul’s message about Jesus was revolutionary. Paul proclaimed that Jesus is not distant—that He is God incarnate, fully divine and fully engaged with humanity. Jesus is the personal, involved God the Epicureans were missing.

The Stoics taught that self-sufficiency and control over emotions were keys to happiness. Paul challenged this idea by declaring that completeness is found in Christ, not in human effort or virtue. True peace and wholeness come not through self-control but through a relationship with Jesus.

The Epicureans and Stoics offered incomplete solutions to life’s problems. In Colossians, Paul presented Jesus as the ultimate answer—the personal, involved God who makes us complete. In Him, we find what philosophy can never provide: fullness of life and peace.
