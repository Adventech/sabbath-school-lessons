### inVite

Muslims often find the Christian concept of atonement challenging as it differs significantly from Islamic teachings. In Islam, Allah grants forgiveness in direct response to the sinner’s sincere repentance, prayer, and good deeds. There is no need for a mediator or sacrifice. Surah Al-Zumar 39:53 emphasizes God’s mercy: “Say, ‘O My servants who have transgressed against themselves [by sinning], do not despair of the mercy of Allah. Indeed, Allah forgives all sins. Indeed, it is He who is the Forgiving, the Merciful.’ ” However, the Bible teaches that sin is not merely a mistake or a broken rule but a moral and relational breach with God. Sin creates a debt, not just in a legal sense but in a relational one as well. Our disobedience fractures the relationship with our Creator. Romans 6:23 states, “For the wages of sin is death.” Sin leads to both spiritual and physical death—to eternal separation from God, and this debt cannot be simply overlooked without compromising God’s justice and holiness. Muslims may struggle to grasp the cost of forgiveness because Islam does not emphasize humanity’s sinfulness in the same way Christianity does. Islam teaches that humans are weak but capable of repentance and reform.

From a Christian perspective, sin causes a relational fracture that requires more than just forgiveness; it requires cleansing and restitution to repair the damage caused by breaking God’s moral law. Isaiah 59:2 says, “Your iniquities have separated you from your God; and your sins have hidden His face from you.” This separation from God is why atonement is necessary to restore the broken relationship and repair the breach.

God’s atonement is complete. It includes both forgiveness and cleansing from sin. However, the atonement is provided at a terrible cost. If God were to forgive without addressing the moral debt, His justice would be compromised. For both justice and mercy to be upheld, a sacrifice is necessary. The biblical understanding is that Jesus, as both fully God and fully human, took on this debt and paid the penalty of sin with His death on the cross.  Hebrews emphasizes that there was no easier way for God to offer forgiveness to the sinner, for “without the shedding of blood there is no forgiveness of sins” (Heb. 9:22, ESV). This verse demonstrates that forgiveness comes at a great cost—one that God Himself paid on our behalf. Knowing this, the cross becomes the ultimate expression of both God’s justice toward sin and evil and His mercy toward the sinner.

`How does believing in a costly forgiveness impact your view of grace?`
