### inTerpret

An apologist engages in various settings—classrooms, public forums, churches, personal conversations, etc.—to present information that removes intellectual barriers to faith and leads people to the truth found in Jesus. Being effective requires staying up to date on the most current apologetic material and presenting it in a culturally relevant way. Proverbs 15:28 says, “The heart of the righteous studies how to answer.” Staying informed on the most recent research is essential. Using outdated or inaccurate sources weakens our defense of the faith and can leave us unprepared against newer objections or evidence. We must read widely, understand effective communication strategies, and stay connected with God on a personal level so our witness comes from a place of relationship rather than facts. Here are three effective strategies:

**Understand presuppositions.** Human communication is a mix of words, motives, and underlying beliefs. False ideas about God often come from faulty presuppositions, which are assumptions that form the foundation of our beliefs. For example, when your car breaks down, the problem might not be obvious. Unseen but crucial parts of the engine might cause the entire system to malfunction. Likewise, erroneous presuppositions can lead to biased conclusions, even if the reasoning process is sound. Identifying and addressing these often-unspoken assumptions is key to dismantling barriers to faith.

**Question the questioner.** Jesus frequently used questions to disarm prejudices and help people rethink their positions. For example, in Luke 2, twelve-year-old Jesus engaged with the teachers in the temple by listening and asking questions, “And all who heard Him were astonished at His understanding and answers” (v. 47). Questions can draw people into deeper reflection and challenge their assumptions without being confrontational. This technique allows the listener to reconsider their stance while feeling heard and respected.

**Paint big ideas about God.** Apologetics isn’t about overwhelming people with lots of Bible verses or slogging through a bunch of details, especially when they may not even believe in the Bible. Instead, focus on the ideas underlying biblical truths and their relevance to daily life. For example, discussing Jesus’ second coming isn’t just about quoting verses to prove how and when it will happen—it’s about the grand concept that God is coming to end suffering and take His people home. The Sabbath can be presented as an opportunity for reconnecting with the One who brings true rest amid the overwhelm of life. Explaining the “why” behind biblical doctrines makes them more accessible and meaningful to everyone, even non-believers. The major themes of the Bible are simple and elegant. Apologetics is not about winning arguments but engaging minds and hearts, presenting truth clearly, and pointing people to God’s love. Focus on the central themes of the Bible and connect on a personal level.
