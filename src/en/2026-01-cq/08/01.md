**Read This Lesson’s Passage:** John 14:5–11

### inTro

**Dinner with Jesus, Buddha, and Muhammad**

Imagine a gathering where some of the world’s most influential religious teachers meet. There stands Muhammad, known for his teachings in the Quran and his role as the prophet of Islam. Beside him is Buddha, who left a life of luxury to seek enlightenment and teach the path to Nirvana. Krishna stands with them, celebrated for his teachings in the Bhagavad Gita, offering wisdom on duty, righteousness, and devotion. Each of these figures brought profound insights and foundational teachings that shaped their respective faiths and millions of lives.

Here’s the thing: some people have the idea that all religions are the same. However, all religions fundamentally disagree about who God is and how people should approach Him. Though there is common ground that can be identified between various religions, they also hold contradictory ideas and beliefs that cannot be reconciled. Each one has a unique set of solutions to the evil in this world.

Buddha would say the Eightfold Path is the way out of suffering. Nirvana can be reached in this life through self-effort. Buddha’s desire was to bring “enlightenment.”

Muhammad would state that salvation is accomplished by practicing the Five Pillars, which are fasting, pilgrimage, giving alms, prayer, and confessing that Muhammad is the final prophet. Muhammad wanted to bring this “way” to the world.

Krishna, in the Bhagavad Gita, emphasized the importance of attaining moksha, or liberation from the cycle of birth and death. He taught that this cycle of reincarnation will continue until a person’s “karmic debt” is paid.

Then enters Jesus. At first glance, He might seem similar—another teacher with wisdom and moral guidance. However, as the discussions unfold, His uniqueness becomes unmistakably clear. Unlike the others, Jesus claims not merely to teach the way but to be the way, the truth, and the life (John 14:6). More than enlightenment, Jesus brings revelation and truth. Instead of constant rebirth, Jesus offers a new birth. Rather than demanding payment of karmic debt, He provides forgiveness. Beyond religious rites, Jesus offers rest.

C.S. Lewis, in Mere Christianity, highlights this uniqueness: “A man who was merely a man and said the sort of things Jesus said would not be a great moral teacher. He would either be a lunatic—on a level with the man who says he is a poached egg—or else he would be the Devil of Hell. You must make your choice. Either this man was, and is, the Son of God: or else a madman or something worse.”<sup>1</sup> Lewis’ statement forces us to confront the reality of Jesus’ claims: He was not simply offering wisdom or spiritual practices; He was declaring Himself to be God.

Beyond the historical evidence, Jesus’ fulfillment of Old Testament prophecies also sets Him apart. His life fulfills scriptures written centuries before His birth, such as Micah 5:2, which foretells the Messiah’s birth in Bethlehem, and Isaiah 53 and Psalm 22, which describe a suffering servant who would be rejected and bear the sins of many. The biographical details foretold in the messianic prophecies, plus the timeline of events foretold in Daniel 9:24–27, perfectly align with the life of Jesus. No other religious leader can claim such prophetic validation. Jesus was not just another religious teacher, but the unique Son of God who came to save humanity.

In the next chapters, we turn to the historical evidence for Jesus’ existence, explore the biblical principles and contextual details of His life, and address common apologetic questions. By comparing Jesus to other religious leaders and understanding His unique claims and fulfilled prophecy, we will see why He stands unparalleled in history.
